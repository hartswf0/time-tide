Problem Statement

Identifying the systemic issues requiring collective action

Creative Dispossession in the Subscription Economy

The transition from ownership to subscription models in creative software has created a systemic problem of creative dispossession. Creators no longer own their tools but instead rent conditional access, fundamentally altering the relationship between creators and their means of production.

Key Dimensions of the Problem:

  • Temporal Dispossession: Creators' access to their tools and past work is contingent on continued payment, creating precarity in creative practice.
  • Format Enclosure: Proprietary file formats create dependency relationships that persist beyond subscription periods, effectively holding creative work hostage.
  • Knowledge Capture: Skills become tied to specific proprietary interfaces rather than transferable conceptual understanding.
  • Educational Pipeline: Educational institutions train students on proprietary tools, creating early dependency and limiting exploration of alternatives.

This problem requires collective action because individual creators lack the bargaining power to influence corporate policies, while educational and cultural institutions have become entangled in proprietary ecosystems through institutional agreements and workflow dependencies.

Target System

Examining the aspect of the social system requiring modification

Educational-Industrial Pipeline in Creative Technology

This policy proposal targets the educational-industrial pipeline that perpetuates dependency on proprietary creative software systems. This pipeline consists of three interconnected components that together form a self-reinforcing system:

Educational Institutions

Universities, colleges, and technical schools that train creative professionals often structure curricula around proprietary tools through:

  • Exclusive licensing agreements with software companies
  • Curriculum designed around specific proprietary interfaces
  • Faculty trained primarily in industry-standard proprietary tools
  • Accreditation requirements that prioritize industry alignment

Industry Employers

Creative industries that hire graduates and set de facto standards through:

  • Workflow dependencies on proprietary file formats
  • Job descriptions requiring specific proprietary tool proficiency
  • Enterprise licensing agreements that normalize subscription costs
  • Legacy systems that resist format migration

Software Providers

Companies that develop and market creative software tools, maintaining market dominance through:

  • Educational licensing programs that create early dependency
  • Proprietary file formats that create lock-in effects
  • Subscription models that increase switching costs
  • Vertical integration of creative workflows
  • Acquisition of potential competitors

This system requires modification because it creates a closed loop that systematically reduces creator sovereignty while increasing dependency. The reinforcing nature of these relationships makes individual action insufficient—students must learn the tools employers require, employers depend on the tools where their workflows exist, and software providers leverage this dependency to maintain their position.

By targeting this educational-industrial pipeline, the policy aims to create intervention points that can disrupt the self-reinforcing cycle and create space for alternative creative ecosystems to emerge and thrive.

Institutional Vehicle

Identifying the institution best suited to execute the policy intervention

National Endowment for Creative Technology (NECT)

This policy proposal recommends the establishment of a new federal agency—the National Endowment for Creative Technology (NECT)—as the primary institutional vehicle for intervention. This agency would operate at the intersection of education, cultural production, and technological development, with a specific mandate to foster creative sovereignty.

Justification for New Institution:

Existing institutions are insufficient for this intervention because:

  • Department of Education lacks specific focus on creative technology and industry connections
  • National Endowment for the Arts has limited technological expertise and funding scope
  • Federal Trade Commission can address market concentration but not educational or cultural dimensions
  • Private foundations lack regulatory authority and sustainable funding mechanisms

Organizational Structure

The NECT would be structured with three primary divisions, each addressing a key aspect of the target system:

Educational Division

  • Develops curriculum standards for sovereign creative technology
  • Provides grants for educational institutions adopting open standards
  • Funds faculty training in open-source creative tools
  • Creates certification programs for technological sovereignty

Infrastructure Division

  • Funds development of open-source creative software
  • Maintains public repositories of open file formats
  • Develops migration tools for proprietary formats
  • Creates technical standards for interoperability

Industry Division

  • Provides tax incentives for businesses adopting open standards
  • Develops procurement guidelines for government creative services
  • Creates industry partnerships for apprenticeship programs
  • Funds research on sustainable business models for open creative tools

Funding and Authority

The NECT would be established with:

  • Initial five-year funding of $500 million annually, drawn from a combination of general appropriations and a small fee on enterprise software licenses
  • Authority to set standards for federal procurement of creative services
  • Grant-making authority for educational institutions and open-source development
  • Regulatory coordination with FTC on format interoperability requirements

This institutional vehicle is uniquely suited for the intervention because it combines educational influence, infrastructure development, and industry incentives—addressing all three components of the target system simultaneously. Its federal status provides the necessary authority and funding stability, while its specialized focus ensures the technical expertise required for effective intervention.

Equilibrium Parameters

Defining the desired conditions for a stable and sovereign creative ecosystem

Balanced Creative Technology Ecosystem

The policy aims to establish and maintain a balanced creative technology ecosystem characterized by the following equilibrium parameters:

Format Diversity and Interoperability

  • Target Parameter: No single proprietary format should exceed 40% market share in any creative domain
  • Interoperability Standard: All formats with >10% market share must provide open documentation and royalty-free conversion paths
  • Preservation Requirement: All formats must include specifications for long-term archival access

Educational Sovereignty

  • Curriculum Balance: Accredited programs must dedicate at least 30% of technical training to non-proprietary tools and formats
  • Conceptual Focus: Assessment criteria must prioritize transferable skills over software-specific techniques
  • Licensing Transparency: Educational institutions must disclose all software licensing agreements and alternatives considered

Market Structure

  • Concentration Limits: No single entity should control more than 50% of tools in any creative domain
  • Business Model Diversity: At least 25% of professionally viable tools should operate on non-subscription models
  • Public Infrastructure: Core creative capabilities should have at least one viable open-source implementation

Temporal Sovereignty Metrics

Drawing directly from the Adobe Killer project's philosophical framework, the policy establishes specific metrics for temporal sovereignty:

Sovereignty Dimension Current State Target Parameter Measurement Method
Access Persistence Contingent on subscription Minimum 10-year access guarantee regardless of payment status License agreement analysis
Format Longevity Proprietary control All formats must have open specifications or escrow arrangements Format registry audit
Knowledge Transferability Interface-specific skills 80% of educational assessments based on conceptual rather than tool-specific skills Curriculum review
Update Autonomy Forced updates User control over update timing and version persistence Software policy analysis

These equilibrium parameters are designed not to eliminate proprietary software—which would create a different form of imbalance—but to ensure that creative ecosystems maintain sufficient diversity, interoperability, and user agency to prevent dependency relationships that undermine sovereignty.

The parameters acknowledge that different creative domains may require different balances, but establish minimum thresholds across all domains to ensure that creators maintain fundamental temporal and technical sovereignty over their work and tools.

Feedback Mechanisms

Monitoring and adjusting system variables to preserve creative sovereignty

Adaptive Governance Framework

To maintain the desired equilibrium parameters, the policy establishes a comprehensive feedback system that monitors key indicators and triggers appropriate interventions when thresholds are crossed. This system operates across multiple timescales and incorporates both quantitative metrics and qualitative assessment.

Continuous Monitoring Systems

  • Format Registry: Tracks market share, interoperability, and preservation status of creative file formats
  • Educational Assessment Database: Analyzes curriculum content and assessment methods across accredited programs
  • Market Concentration Observatory: Monitors ownership patterns and business models in creative technology
  • Creator Sovereignty Index: Surveys creators annually on their perceived autonomy and dependency

Intervention Triggers and Mechanisms

When monitoring indicates deviation from equilibrium parameters, the following feedback mechanisms activate:

Educational Interventions

  • Trigger: Curriculum analysis shows >80% focus on proprietary tools
  • Response: Targeted grants for open-source curriculum development
  • Mechanism: Conditional accreditation requirements
  • Timeframe: Implementation within 2 academic years

Format Interventions

  • Trigger: Format exceeds 40% market share without open documentation
  • Response: Accelerated funding for alternative format development
  • Mechanism: Interoperability requirements for government procurement
  • Timeframe: 18-month compliance window

Market Structure Interventions

  • Trigger: Single entity controls >50% of tools in a creative domain
  • Response: Enhanced funding for competitive alternatives
  • Mechanism: Coordination with antitrust authorities on merger review
  • Timeframe: Strategic investment over 3-5 year horizon

Cyclical Evaluation Process

Drawing from the Adobe Killer project's concept of "folding time," the policy implements a cyclical evaluation process that connects past, present, and future states of the creative ecosystem:

Cycle Activities Outputs
Quarterly Data collection from monitoring systems Early warning indicators report
Annual Comprehensive analysis of all parameters State of Creative Sovereignty Report
Biennial Stakeholder congress with representatives from education, industry, and creator communities Parameter adjustment recommendations
Five-Year Full policy review and strategic planning Updated equilibrium parameters and intervention mechanisms

This multi-layered feedback system ensures that the policy remains responsive to changing technological conditions while maintaining consistent progress toward the core goal of creative sovereignty. By incorporating both rapid-response mechanisms and longer-term strategic adjustments, the system can address immediate imbalances while adapting to emerging trends in creative technology.

Crucially, the feedback mechanisms themselves operate with transparency and participatory governance, ensuring that the policy does not simply replace one form of control with another, but genuinely increases creator agency within the system.

Risks & Consequences

Analyzing potential unintended effects and mitigation strategies

Potential Risks of Intervention

While the policy aims to increase creative sovereignty, several potential risks and unintended consequences must be considered:

Innovation Disruption

Risk: Regulatory requirements could potentially slow the pace of innovation in creative tools by imposing compliance costs.

Mitigation: Implement tiered requirements based on market size, with lighter regulation for emerging tools and startups. Focus interoperability requirements on file formats rather than features.

Market Exit

Risk: Major software providers could withdraw from certain markets rather than comply with interoperability requirements.

Mitigation: Phase implementation gradually and provide technical assistance for compliance. Ensure robust alternatives exist before enforcing stricter requirements.

Educational Disruption

Risk: Rapid curriculum changes could create transitional challenges for students and faculty.

Mitigation: Implement changes over multiple academic cycles with bridge programs for students caught in transitions. Provide faculty development resources.

Quality Disparities

Risk: Open alternatives may initially lack feature parity with established proprietary tools.

Mitigation: Prioritize funding for core functionality development and establish quality benchmarks for funded projects. Create migration pathways that acknowledge feature differences.

Paradoxical Consequences

Drawing from the philosophical framework of the Adobe Killer project, the policy acknowledges several potential paradoxes that require careful navigation:

The Sovereignty Paradox

Mandating specific approaches to sovereignty could itself become a form of control that limits creator autonomy. The policy addresses this by:

  • Focusing on expanding options rather than eliminating choices
  • Emphasizing transparency and disclosure over prohibition
  • Including creator representatives in governance structures
  • Establishing sunset provisions for interventions to prevent permanent regulatory capture

The Infrastructure Paradox

Public funding for creative tools could create new forms of dependency on government support. The policy addresses this by:

  • Developing multiple sustainable funding models beyond direct government support
  • Distributing funding through diverse channels to prevent centralized control
  • Requiring governance models for funded projects that ensure independence
  • Supporting research on community-sustained infrastructure

Equilibrium Shifts and System Stability

The policy intervention could trigger several system-wide adjustments that must be anticipated:

Potential Shift Impact Assessment Stability Considerations
Business Model Evolution Companies may shift to hybrid models combining open and proprietary elements Could create more sustainable ecosystem if open components receive sufficient investment
Educational Specialization Some institutions may specialize in proprietary tools while others focus on open alternatives Beneficial if transparent to students, problematic if creates two-tier system
International Divergence Different regulatory approaches across countries could fragment creative ecosystems Requires international coordination and standards harmonization
Technological Adaptation AI and cloud technologies may evolve to circumvent traditional format-based regulations Policy must maintain technological neutrality while addressing underlying sovereignty principles

By anticipating these risks and potential consequences, the policy can implement adaptive measures that preserve its core objectives while responding to emerging challenges. The goal is not a rigid implementation of specific technologies or approaches, but a dynamic system that continuously rebalances toward greater creative sovereignty.

As the Adobe Killer project demonstrates through its practical implementation of slit-scan technology, liberation is not achieved through rejection alone, but through the creation of viable alternatives that expand creative possibilities. This policy aims to create the conditions for many such alternatives to emerge and thrive.
