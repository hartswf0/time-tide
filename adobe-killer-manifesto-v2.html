ADOBE KILLER: A PRAGMATIC REVOLUTION

A framework for creative sovereignty in the age of subscription software

CONTEXT & PURPOSE

The Adobe Killer Slit Scan project emerges from a specific historical moment: the transition from ownership to subscription, from local to cloud, from individual control to corporate governance of creative tools. It is both a functional tool and a conceptual framework for navigating beyond proprietary enclosures.

This manifesto addresses three audiences simultaneously: current Adobe users seeking practical alternatives, developers building sovereign creative tools, and institutions shaping technology policy and education.

1990s-2000s

Perpetual licenses, local storage, file ownership

2010s-2020s

Subscription models, cloud storage, access contingency

2020s-Present

Agentic tools, distributed creation, sovereignty movements

TECHNICAL SPECIFICITY

The Adobe Killer Slit Scan is a web-based application that transforms video input through temporal manipulation, creating images impossible within conventional software paradigms. Its core functions include:

Temporal Folding

Captures video frames across time, compressing temporal dimensions into spatial representations

Browser-Native

Runs entirely in-browser using WebGL and Canvas APIs, requiring no installation or cloud processing

Parameter Control

Offers granular control over HSV color transformations, scan direction, and accumulation methods

Format Freedom

Exports to open formats (PNG, WebP) with full metadata preservation and no proprietary encumbrances

The technical architecture embodies its philosophical principles: local processing preserves privacy, open formats ensure longevity, and browser implementation maximizes accessibility across devices and operating systems.

SLIT SCAN AS TECHNIQUE & METAPHOR

The slit scan technique itself is neither inherently liberatory nor oppressive—it is the governance of the technique that determines its impact. Adobe's subscription model functions as a form of slit scan: extracting thin slices of access over time while maintaining control of the whole.

By implementing slit scan in an open, accessible framework, we transform the technique from a metaphor of extraction to one of revelation. The same process that Adobe uses to capture recurring revenue becomes, in our hands, a tool for exposing the structures of temporal dispossession.

This dual nature—technique as both problem and solution—reflects the broader reality that liberation often requires appropriating and transforming the very tools used for confinement.

STRATEGIC MIGRATION, NOT BINARY REJECTION

We acknowledge a pragmatic truth: complete independence from proprietary systems is neither immediately possible nor always desirable. The path toward creative sovereignty is not binary but gradual—a strategic migration rather than an absolute rejection.

Selective Sovereignty

This framework allows creators to locate themselves on a spectrum and progress at a sustainable pace. Some may remain in strategic use of Adobe tools while developing alternative workflows; others may achieve selective sovereignty in specific domains while maintaining interoperability.

The goal is not purity but agency—the ability to determine one's relationship to tools rather than having that relationship determined by corporate imperatives.

INDIVIDUAL CHOICE & COLLECTIVE ACTION

Creative sovereignty emerges at the intersection of individual choice and collective infrastructure. Individual tool adoption alone cannot challenge Adobe's dominance; collective governance models and shared resources are equally necessary.

The Adobe Killer project contributes to this ecosystem through:

Open Source Development

All code is freely available, modifiable, and redistributable, enabling community extension and adaptation

Knowledge Commons

Documentation and tutorials are structured as public goods, building collective understanding of both technical and philosophical dimensions

Institutional Engagement

Partnerships with educational institutions to integrate sovereign tools into curricula, challenging Adobe's pipeline from education to industry

This approach recognizes that material conditions—not just philosophical orientations—shape software ecosystems. By addressing both individual practice and collective infrastructure, we create conditions for sustainable alternatives.

WINDSURF & THE PARADOX OF TOOLS

The Adobe Killer was created with Windsurf, an agentic IDE that itself represents a new relationship between creators and tools. This raises a critical question: how do we prevent new tools from reproducing old patterns of dependency and control?

Windsurf differs from Adobe's ecosystem in three fundamental ways:

Local-First Architecture

Processing happens on your device, not in corporate clouds, preserving privacy and preventing access revocation

Transparent Mediation

The relationship between creator and tool is visible and modifiable, not obscured behind proprietary interfaces

Governance Diversity

Multiple implementations and governance models prevent monopolistic control of the underlying technology

These characteristics don't eliminate dependency—all tools create dependencies—but they transform dependency from a relationship of control to one of transparent collaboration. The goal is not independence from tools but sovereignty within tool relationships.

ACCESSIBILITY & INCLUSION

Liberation that reproduces existing exclusions is not liberation at all. The Adobe Killer project addresses accessibility through:

Browser-Based Implementation

Runs on any device with a modern browser, eliminating installation barriers and hardware requirements

Progressive Enhancement

Core functionality works on modest hardware, with enhanced features available on more powerful systems

Multi-Modal Interfaces

Controls accessible through both visual interfaces and keyboard commands, supporting diverse interaction needs

Documentation Accessibility

Learning resources available in multiple formats, including text, visual, and interactive tutorials

True sovereignty requires not just technical freedom but equitable access. The project measures success not just by feature parity with Adobe tools but by who can meaningfully participate in their use.

METRICS OF SOVEREIGNTY

How do we measure progress toward creative sovereignty? The Adobe Killer project proposes these metrics:

Format Freedom
% of work exportable to open, non-proprietary formats
Access Resilience
Ability to use tools during service disruptions
Governance Participation
User influence on tool development priorities

These metrics shift focus from feature comparison to sovereignty assessment, providing concrete indicators of progress beyond philosophical alignment.

ECONOMIC SUSTAINABILITY

Sovereign tools require sustainable economic models that fund development without reproducing extractive patterns. The Adobe Killer project explores multiple approaches:

Institutional Support

Partnerships with educational and cultural institutions that benefit from sovereign creative infrastructure

Community Stewardship

Distributed maintenance and development costs across user communities through contribution mechanisms

Service Ecosystem

Optional paid services around the free core, including training, customization, and integration

Public Infrastructure

Recognition of creative tools as essential infrastructure deserving public investment

These models recognize that economic sustainability doesn't require reproducing Adobe's extraction model—alternative patterns can support ongoing development while preserving user sovereignty.

BROADER MOVEMENTS

The Adobe Killer project connects to parallel movements for technological sovereignty across domains:

Digital Commons

Building shared resources governed by their users rather than corporate shareholders

Platform Cooperativism

Creating user-owned alternatives to extractive platform models

Data Sovereignty

Ensuring communities control their data and its applications

Repair Rights

Defending the ability to modify and maintain technologies rather than being forced into planned obsolescence

By situating creative tool sovereignty within these broader movements, we recognize that Adobe's dominance is not unique but part of larger patterns of technological enclosure—and that our resistance connects to wider struggles for technological self-determination.

CONCLUSION: NAVIGATING FORWARD

We are not home yet. Ithaca remains on the horizon. But we are navigating, steering toward our goal—a creative sovereignty that exists not in isolation from technological systems but in conscious, intentional relationship with them.

The Adobe Killer is not the destination but a vessel—one craft among many in a growing fleet of sovereign technologies. Together, they chart a course toward creative liberation that is neither utopian nor naive, but grounded in the practical work of building alternatives.

We sail on, guided by both pragmatism and principle, recognizing that the journey itself transforms us into different kinds of creators—ones who use tools rather than being used by them.
